The most significant insight from Week 8 is deceptively simple: **bounded random exploration outperformed every sophisticated surrogate model tried over 7 prior weeks on Function 4**. The "breakthrough" was not a discovery of structure — it was a lucky sample in a chaotic landscape that happened to yield a high value.

**The honest argument**: F4's improvement is directionally encouraging, but statistically unreliable. We cannot know if −5.556 is the start of a recoverable trend or another local maximum before another collapse. The decision to query F4 again in Week 9 is a calculated bet, not a guarantee.

### 3.1 Clustering: K-Means and Hierarchical Clustering

**Course context**: K-means partitions a dataset into k clusters by minimizing within-cluster variance. Hierarchical clustering builds a dendrogram of nested groupings and does not require pre-specifying k.

**Relevance to this project**:

In the context of black-box optimization, clustering would serve two roles:

**Role 1: Input space partitioning**  
Rather than sampling uniformly across `[0,1]^D`, we could cluster the already-evaluated query points and use the cluster structure to identify under-explored regions. If a cluster has high density but low output variance, it is likely a plateau and should be de-prioritized. If a cluster has low density but mixed outputs, it is informative and deserves exploration.

*Applied retrospectively*: F5's collapse from 79.327 to 9.247 could have been partially anticipated if W7's query had been placed in a *different cluster* from W6's successful query, rather than adjacent to it. The assumption that "near the peak is better" was exactly what clustering-based diversity sampling would challenge.

**Role 2: Function grouping by behavior type**  
Looking at the 8 functions' performance trajectories across 9 weeks, a k-means clustering of functions by their output sequences would likely produce three natural groups:

| Cluster | Functions | Behavioral Profile |
|:---:|:---:|:---|
| Cluster 1 | F1 | Noise floor — no learnable signal |
| Cluster 2 | F2, F3, F5, F7 | Non-stationary, volatile, or collapsed |
| Cluster 3 | F4, F6, F8 | Exploitable structure, stable or improving |

This grouping validates the W9 allocation decision: **only functions in Cluster 3 should receive budget in the final rounds**. Hierarchical clustering would further reveal that F4 and F8 are the most distant from the unstable cluster, making them the optimal final allocations.

**Limitation**: With only 8–9 time steps per function, clustering is descriptive rather than predictive. The dendrogram would show structure, but we cannot claim that the clusters would remain stable if more queries were added.

### 3.2 Generative AI and Large Language Models (LLMs)

**Course context**: LLMs are neural networks trained on massive text corpora that learn to generate coherent, contextually appropriate outputs given a prompt. Key concepts include token prediction, temperature-controlled sampling, few-shot prompting, and hallucination risk.

**Relevance to this project**:

This project functions as a structural analogy to LLM-based generation — and the analogy becomes richer as we reflect on what went wrong.

**Analogy 1: Temperature and Exploration**  
In LLMs, *temperature* controls output randomness. High temperature → diverse, exploratory outputs. Low temperature → deterministic, concentrated outputs.

In our optimization:
- Weeks 1–3 (high exploration): equivalent to high-temperature sampling. We discovered that F5 had a promising region and F8 was reliable.
- Week 6 (low temperature exploitation): we committed aggressively to F5's peak region. This is exactly what low-temperature LLM generation does — it collapses diversity and can get "stuck" on a locally coherent but globally incorrect output.
- Week 8 (medium temperature): bounded random sampling reintroduced diversity and discovered F4's exploitable region.

**The lesson that applies to both LLMs and Bayesian optimization**: Premature temperature reduction is dangerous when the landscape is not fully characterized. Exploitation without sufficient exploration leads to collapse.

**Analogy 2: Hallucination and Surrogate Overfitting**  
LLMs hallucinate when they generate plausible-sounding but factually incorrect content, particularly when prompted about topics with insufficient training data. Our surrogate models hallucinated in the same way:

- With N=6 observations and D=4 dimensions, the SVM RBF surrogate fit a smooth surface to noise.
- The acquisition function then recommended the "maximum" of a hallucinated landscape.
- The actual function returned a value far below prediction — analogous to a confident but incorrect LLM response.

**Mitigation strategies that apply to both domains**:
1. *Calibrated uncertainty*: Good LLMs return "I don't know" in low-data regimes. Good surrogates should have wide confidence intervals when N << D.
2. *Ensemble disagreement as a warning signal*: When multiple models disagree strongly on a prediction, treat it as a high-uncertainty regime and sample conservatively.
3. *Chain-of-thought reasoning*: Rather than relying on a single surrogate prediction, explicitly reasoning through the history of observations (as done in W8 for F4) produced the most defensible decision.

**Argument for W9**: The F8 query was generated using a *centroid of successful observations* — this is analogous to few-shot prompting, where we provide the model with examples of "what good looks like" and ask it to generate a new response in that region. The F4 query was generated by adding Gaussian noise to a successful prior query — analogous to *temperature-controlled continuation* from a high-quality prefix.

### 3.3 Hyperparameter Tuning

**Course context**: Hyperparameter tuning (HPT) refers to the process of selecting the external configuration of a machine learning model (learning rate, regularization strength, kernel parameters, architecture depth) that is not learned during training. Common methods include grid search, random search, Bayesian optimization, and early stopping.

**Relevance to this project**:

There is a direct and meaningful connection here: **this entire project is, structurally, a hyperparameter tuning problem**. Each function `f_i: [0,1]^D → R` could represent the validation performance of a model as a function of its hyperparameters. The goal — maximize with a limited query budget — is precisely what Bayesian hyperparameter optimization attempts to do.

**Tuning decisions made across weeks**:

| Week | "Hyperparameter" Tuned | Effect |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| W1–W3 | Sampling strategy (LHS vs. random) | Established diverse coverage |
| W4–W5 | Surrogate model type (Ridge → SVM → NN) | Marginal improvements, overfitting risk |
| W6 | Acquisition function aggressiveness (ξ in EI) | F5 peak, but unsustainable |
| W7 | Ensemble consensus threshold | Failed — over-complex |
| W8 | Exploration radius (bounds width) | F4 breakthrough via simplification |
| W9 | Allocation (which functions to query) | Portfolio-level hyperparameter decision |

**Key insight**: Hyperparameter tuning itself suffers from the same fundamental problem — the more hyperparameters we tune with limited evaluation budget, the more we risk overfitting to the validation set. **The correct response to extreme budget constraints is to reduce the hyperparameter search space, not expand it**. This was the lesson from W8: use a fixed, simple strategy (`uniform([0.35, 0.65])`) rather than a tuned ensemble.

**Argument for W9**: The W9 queries were generated with deliberately minimal hyperparameter decisions — no acquisition function tuning, no ensemble weighting, no kernel selection. The rationale is that with 2 queries remaining, any additional tuning introduces more variance than it reduces. This mirrors the recommendation to use **random search over grid search** in HPT when the evaluation budget is very small: random search explores more of the space with fewer total evaluations.

## 4. Honest Assessment and Uncertainty

### 4.1 What We Cannot Claim

- We cannot claim to have found the global optimum of any function.
- We cannot claim that F4's improvement will continue — one good result in a chaotic landscape is not a validated trend.
- We cannot claim that the surrogate models provide accurate predictions — with N < 20, all confidence intervals should be treated with skepticism.
- We cannot claim to have outperformed random search in most functions: for F1, F2, F3, F5, F6, F7, the evidence suggests that uniform random sampling would have performed similarly or better.

### 4.2 What We Can Claim

- We demonstrated principled decision-making under radical uncertainty.
- We correctly identified non-stationarity and noise floors in 5 of 8 functions and abandoned them before they consumed remaining budget.
- We used ensemble disagreement as a genuine uncertainty signal, not just as a formality.
- We connected empirical observations (F4 breakthrough via simplicity, F5 collapse via overexploitation) to course-level theory in a way that generalizes beyond this specific challenge.
- The portfolio allocation in W9 (F4 + F8) is a defensible risk-adjusted decision, not an arbitrary choice.
